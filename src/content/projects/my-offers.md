<h3 class="section-title">The Mobile Native Offer Ecosystem</h3>
</div>
<div class="section-intro-col">
<p class="section-intro">Responding to a parity gap with an iterative, strategic architecture.</p>
</div>
<div class="section-body-col">
<p>The web experience featured a dedicated space for targeted user offers, but the mobile application lacked this critical real estate. This discrepancy forced a reliance on oversaturated outbound marketing channels, resulting in measurable email fatigue. As the lead product designer, my objective was not simply to replicate the web feature, but to architect a native mobile experience from the ground up. This required designing for current data while simultaneously planning for the influx of offers that would eventually route to this screen.</p>

<h3 class="section-title">Orchestrating Cross Functional Alignment</h3>
</div>
<div class="section-intro-col">
<p class="section-intro">Driving consensus by leaning into strategy and collaborative workshops.</p>
</div>
<div class="section-body-col">
<p>Integrating offers into the core application impacted numerous product and business verticals. This required deep collaboration with different team leads and a dedicated product strategist. Together, we facilitated interactive workshops, using Miro to build alignment on requirements. To keep the project moving between meetings, I established asynchronous feedback loops in Figma. This proactive communication structure was vital for maintaining visibility, gathering diverse perspectives without meeting fatigue, and ensuring everyone felt ownership of the process.</p>

<section class="case-study-section">
<div class="section-header-col">
<h3 class="section-title">Designing for System Discrepancies</h3>
</div>
<div class="section-intro-col">
<p class="section-intro">Navigating conflicting design system versions without technical bloat.</p>
</div>
<div class="section-body-col">
<p>The web "My Offers" page was built on the legacy v1 design system, while the mobile app was operating on the newer v2 library. The components were not 1:1. Demanding new v2 components built from scratch would have significantly inflated development time. I collaborated periodically with the design system team, using my visual proposals as the basis for workarounds. We adapted existing v2 components to mimic essential legacy treatments, protecting the engineering timeline while still maintaining the intended editorial rhythm.</p>

<section class="case-study-section">
<div class="section-header-col">
<h3 class="section-title">Strategic Roadmapping</h3>
</div>
<div class="section-intro-col">
<p class="section-intro">Defending iterative deployment and defining the next product evolution.</p>
</div>
<div class="section-body-col">
<p>A core part of lead level decision making is knowing when not to build. The design explorations naturally included ideal future states, like sophisticated filtering. However, for launch, with only a handful of offers to display, investing development time in filtering did not make product sense. My proposed designs, shared as a strategic "North Star," clearly articulated how the interface would evolve once the screen demonstrated traction and other teams routed their 40 plus offers to it.</p>

<section class="case-study-section">
<div class="section-header-col">
<h3 class="section-title">Legal Advocacy & UX Optimization</h3>
</div>
<div class="section-intro-col">
<p class="section-intro">Streamlining user flows through deep legal collaboration and trust.</p>
</div>
<div class="section-body-col">
<p>In a highly regulated environment, legal approval is a critical stakeholder partnership. I drove a focused UX decision: advocating for the removal of the enrollment checkbox on the offers screen, as users had already signed terms on a previous page. I jumped on calls with eight lawyers, presenting our rationale via custom legal review decks. Through responsive collaboration and a readiness to provide clear, logic based explanations for my proposals, I fostered genuine trust. By addressing compliance concerns at the root and demonstrating responsiveness to their feedback, we not only streamlined the mobile experience but also successfully advocated for the same change to be implemented back in the web platform, improving it there too.</p>

<section class="case-study-section">
<div class="section-header-col">
<h3 class="section-title">Execution of Accessibility Standards</h3>
</div>
<div class="section-intro-col">
<p class="section-intro">Implementing required screen reader protocols and bespoke legal accommodations.</p>
</div>
<div class="section-body-col">
<p>Financial disclosures inherently carry a high cognitive load. To ensure equitable access, I generated comprehensive accessibility annotations dictating the reading sequence, focus order, and screen reader behavior to meet standard compliance. The offer cards were structured to allow users to navigate high level context via keyboard without parsing entire text blocks. During compliance reviews, one specific legal partner required non visual accommodations. I authored a parallel plain text document mirroring the architectural structure solely for his review process. I executed these standards and partnered with an internal accessibility expert who validated the final output against AA compliance.</p>

<section class="case-study-section">
<div class="section-header-col">
<h3 class="section-title">Iconography & Visual Hierarchy</h3>
</div>
<div class="section-intro-col">
<p class="section-intro">Meticulous visual optimization for mobile readability.</p>
</div>
<div class="section-body-col">
<p>To mitigate cognitive load and ensure WCAG compliance, the visual interface required precise craftsmanship. I audited and modified existing iconography to ensure minimum tap target sizes were met for the mobile viewport. Furthermore, the application of color and typographic hierarchy was strictly calibrated. By prioritizing character counts and horizontal line lengths within the mobile grid, the design maintained an editorial rhythm that guided the user through complex financial decisions without overwhelming them.</p>

<section class="case-study-section">
<div class="section-header-col">
<h3 class="section-title">Retrospective on Dual Leadership</h3>
</div>
<div class="section-intro-col">
<p class="section-intro">Defining operational boundaries to execute without a formal product manager.</p>
</div>
<div class="section-body-col">
<p>The absence of a dedicated product manager required a strict division of labor between the product strategist and myself. The strategist owned operational alignment, schedule management, and cross functional onboarding by leveraging her prior context from the web platform launch. My focus remained entirely on architectural execution and stakeholder socialization. I owned the design delivery, presented the design rationale, managed the iterative feedback loops, and drove the technical partnerships with the design system and accessibility teams. This explicit boundary allowed us to maintain absolute consistency in our messaging across large stakeholder groups. This ensured the project shipped on schedule without scope creep or timeline delays.</p>

<section class="case-study-section">

<h3 class="section-title">Measurable Business Impact</h3>
</div>
<div class="section-intro-col">
<p class="section-intro">Proving a new channel value by reaching previously lost audiences.</p>
</div>
<div class="section-body-col">
<p>The project launch successfully unlocked a powerful new lifecycle touchpoint. By reaching the Email Ineligible user segment, users unreachable through traditional outbound marketing, the interface unlocked a previously inaccessible demographic. Data validated the approach: in the first quarter, this native digital home generated $773,000 in incremental sales from this segment alone, with a 3.4% actual enrollment rate. The design proved that a calibrated digital experience can directly recover value from audiences lost to traditional marketing.</p>
